    Capital Market Instruments

    Equity Shares

    • Part of total capital (Companies Act, 1956)
    • Entitled to dividend and voting right
    • Bear risk of corporate performance
    • Issued in primary market; traded in secondary via stock exchanges

    Preference Shares

    • Limited risk; predetermined dividend
    • No voting rights
    • Priority in capital repayment over equity shareholders
    • Marketable but not widely traded (fixed dividend)

    Bonds/Debentures

    • Debt instruments; fixed interest bearing
    • Issued by corporates, govts, DFIs
    • Negotiable; transferable by endorsement
    • Can be issued with or without security

    • Internationally traded equity instruments against equity shares
    • Fixed ratio of Indian shares quoted on BSE
    • Issued by international depository
    • Denominated in US Dollars

    ADR

    • Similar to GDR; traded in US markets
    • Denominated in US Dollars
    • Under FCCB & Ordinary Shares (Depository Receipt Mechanism) Scheme, 1993

    Financial Markets

    Classification and all types

    Definition

    • Market where entities trade financial securities at low transaction costs at prices reflecting supply & demand
    • Transfers funds from surplus units to deficit units
    • Link between savers and investors — NOT a source of finance
    6 Classification Bases
    BasisTypes
    Maturity of claimsMoney Market (short-term) · Capital Market (long-term)
    Seasoning of claimPrimary Market · Secondary Market
    Structure/ArrangementsOrganised Markets · Unorganised Markets
    Type of financial claimDebt Market · Equity Market
    Timing of deliveryCash/Spot Market · Forward/Future Market
    Other TypesForeign Exchange Market · Derivatives Market

    Organised Markets

    • Standardised rules and regulations
    • High degree of institutionalisation
    • Strict supervision by RBI or regulatory bodies
    • Includes: Capital Market + Money Market

    Unorganised Markets

    • Money lenders, indigenous bankers, traders
    • Private finance companies, chit funds
    • Not controlled by RBI
    • RBI issued NBFC (RB) Directions, 1998
    Unit I

    Capital Market

    Structure, importance, primary, secondary markets

    Definition

    • Market for long-term finance (maturity > 1 year)
    • Buying and selling equity, debt and other securities
    • Focus on financing of fixed investments

    Participants

    • Mutual Funds, Insurance organisations
    • Foreign Institutional Investors (FIIs)
    • Corporates and Individuals
    • Issuers, Stock Exchange, Merchant Bankers, Depositories, Stock Brokers, Market Regulator

    6 Importance Points

    • Mobilises savings; avoids wastage in unproductive uses
    • Provides incentive to saving; facilitates capital formation through rates of interest
    • Avenue for investors (particularly household sector) in financial assets more productive than physical assets
    • Facilitates increase in production and productivity → enhances economic welfare
    • Promotes economic growth
    • Stability in values of securities representing capital funds

    Indian Capital Market Structure

    • Government Securities Market (Gilt-Edged)
    • Industrial Securities Market → New Issues Market + Secondary Market
    • Development Financial Institutions: IFCI, ICICI, SFCs, IDBI, IIBI, UTI
    • Financial Intermediaries: Merchant Banks, Mutual Funds, Leasing Companies, Venture Capital Companies, Others

    Industrial Securities Market

    • Market for shares and debentures of existing and new Corporate firms
    • Instruments: Equity shares, Preference shares, Debentures/Bonds
    • Sub-divided into: Primary Market (New Issue Market) + Secondary Market (Stock Exchange)

    Primary / New Issue Market

    • Market for new issues — fresh capital; securities issued to public for first time
    • Does not have any organisational setup
    • Both new and existing companies can issue here
    • Performs triple-service function: Origination, Underwriting, Distribution
    • Facilitates capital formation

    3 Methods of Raising Capital

    • Public Issue: Issuing shares to the general public
    • Rights Issue: Issuing shares to the existing shareholders
    • Private Placement: Issuing shares to selected group of investors based on pre-agreement

    Secondary Market / Stock Market

    • Market for old issues — buying and selling of already-issued securities
    • Operates through Stock Exchanges
    • Regulated under Securities Contracts (Regulation) Act, 1956
    • Bombay Stock Exchange (BSE) is the principal stock exchange in India

    Government Securities Market (Gilt-Edged)

    • Long-term Govt securities traded here; short-term traded in money market
    • Securities of: Central Govt, State Govts, Semi-Govt authorities (City Corporations, Port Trusts, State Electricity Boards), All India & State-level FIs, Public Sector Enterprises

    Long-term Loans Market — 3 Sub-markets

    • Term Loans Market: Industrial financing institutions; Govt created at national & regional level; help identify investment opportunities, support new entrepreneurs, modernisation. Key: IDBI, IFCI, ICICI, SFCs
    • Mortgages Market: Institutions providing mortgage loans mainly to individuals
    • Financial Guarantees Market: Institutions providing financial guarantees; insurance companies are major participants
    Unit I

    Money Market

    Call money, T-Bills, Commercial Bills, CD, Commercial Paper

    Definition

    • Market for short-term finance (maturity < 1 year)
    • Trades: Trade Bills, Promissory Notes, Short-term Govt Papers
    • Organised money market: RBI, Banks (commercial and co-operative)

    Purpose — Banks Borrow to:

    • Fill temporary gaps / mismatch of funds
    • Meet CRR and SLR mandatory requirements stipulated by RBI
    • Meet sudden demand (e.g. advance tax payments)

    Call Money Market

    • Day-to-day surplus funds of banks traded here
    • Call money: 1 day | Notice money: 2–14 days | Term money: >15 days
    • Exclusively limited to banks temporarily short of funds
    • Clean basis — no collateral required
    • Participants (both borrow & lend): RBI (via LAF), Banks, Primary Dealers
    • Pure inter-bank market since August 2005
    • Highly competitive and sensitive; good indicator of liquidity position

    Treasury Bills (T-Bills)

    • Short-term liabilities of Govt of India
    • Tenors: 91-day, 182-day, 364-day
    • Issued at discount to face value; face value paid at maturity
    • Rate of discount determined at each auction

    Commercial Bills

    • Works like Bill of Exchange
    • Businesses issue for short-term money requirements
    • Not as developed in India as T-Bill market

    Short-term Bank Loans

    • Commercial banks provide overdrafts and cash credits
    • For working capital requirements of traders and industrialists

    Certificate of Deposit (CD)

    • Negotiable term deposit accepted by commercial banks
    • Issued through a promissory note
    • Issued to: individuals, corporations, trusts
    • Can be issued at a discount by scheduled commercial banks
    • Duration: 3 months to 1 year

    Commercial Paper (CP)

    • Issued by corporates for short-term working capital requirements
    • Alternative to borrowing from a bank
    • Period: 15 days to 1 year
    • Issued at discount to face value; market decides discount rate
    • Requires RBI's prior approval
    • Minimum size: Rs. 5 lakhs and multiples thereof
    • Maximum size: 100% of the issuer's working capital

    Unit II

    Banking Institutions

    All 7 types — full definitions

    Banking business = accepting deposits from the public for the purpose of lending or investment, repayable on demand.

    1. Commercial Banks

    • Finance for working capital (day-to-day: raw materials, salaries)
    • Raise funds through various kinds of deposits
    • Lend short and medium-term loans to traders and business persons

    Scheduled Banks (sub-type)

    • Registered in 2nd Schedule of RBI
    • Must carry banking business in India
    • Paid-up capital and reserves ≥ Rs. 5 lakhs
    • Must not conduct affairs detrimental to depositors
    • Under direct purview of RBI credit control; entitled to borrowing & rediscounting

    2. Industrial / Investment Banks

    • Finance for fixed capital (land, building, plant, machinery, furniture)
    • Raise funds through shares, debentures, bonds
    • Lend medium and long-term loans to industrialists

    3. Exchange Banks

    • Specialise in financing foreign trade
    • Deal with foreign exchange transactions

    4. Co-operative Banks

    • Formed on principle of mutual co-operation
    • Members from same activity (farming, retailing, small industry)
    • Lend at generally low rate of interest
    • Significant role in agricultural and allied activities

    5. Land Development Banks

    • Formed by agriculturists on mutual cooperation principle
    • Raise funds through shares, debentures, bonds
    • Loans for tools, equipment, permanent land improvement, cattle farming
    • Now called Agriculture and Rural Development Banks

    6. Regional Rural Banks (RRBs)

    • Govt-sponsored under RRB Act, 1976
    • Shareholding: Central Govt 50% : Sponsoring Bank 35% : State Govt 15%
    • Hybrid: cooperative local orientation + commercial banking culture
    • Customers: small farmers, agricultural labourers, socio-economically weaker sections

    7. Savings Banks

    • Mobilise savings of poor and middle income people
    • Primary objective: promote habit of thrift and savings
    • Restrictions on number & amount of withdrawals per period
    • In India, run through postal department

    Non-Banking Financial Companies (NBFCs)

    Dual classification, services, regulation

    Definition & Key Distinctions from Banks

    • Specialised FI other than a banking institution; mobilises savings of a certain segment; lends for specialised needs
    • Can accept deposits — but NOT demand deposits
    • Cannot issue cheques | Cannot borrow from RBI
    • Flexible structure: tailor-made services
    RBI Classification — 3 Categories

    Asset Financing Companies

    • Finance for physical assets — automobiles, tractors, earth-moving equipment
    • Examples: Bajaj Auto Finance, Shriram Finance, Fullerton India

    Investment Companies

    • Acquire and trade in securities (shares, stocks, bonds, debentures)
    • Pool investment from investors
    • Examples: ICICI Prudential MF, Nippon India MF, HDFC MF

    Loan Companies

    • Give loans — housing loans, gold loans
    • Examples: Muthoot Finance, Manappuram Gold Finance, HDFC, LIC Housing
    General Classification — 4 Categories

    Development Institutions

    • Long-term finance for agriculture and industrial development
    • Multipurpose: project identification, technical advice, underwriting, promotion
    • Examples: IFCI, SIDBI, SFCs, SIDC

    Specialised Institutions

    • Assistance to special sectors: housing, infrastructure, agriculture
    • Generally do NOT accept public deposits
    • Examples: NABARD, NHB, NHAI, EXIM Bank

    Investment Institutions

    • Acquire and trade in securities in capital market
    • Mobilise savings in forms other than demand deposits
    • Examples: LIC, GIC, UTI

    Other Institutions

    • Core Investment Companies
    • Micro Financing Institutions
    • Equipment Leasing Companies
    • Hire Purchasing Finance Companies
    • Housing Finance Companies
    • Nidhi Companies, Chit Fund Companies

    9 Services Rendered by NBFCs

    • Mobilisation of savings through attractive schemes and rates
    • Timely provision of adequate credit facility easily and simply
    • Act as financial supermarket for diversified credit
    • Channelise funds for productive purposes (capital intensive industries)
    • Encourage thrift and savings habit
    • Enable asset creation through housing finance
    • Increase standard of living through instalment facilities for consumer durables
    • Render expert advice on investment of funds
    • Promote economic development by providing wider choice

    Regulation of NBFCs

    • Mandatory: Certificate of registration + minimum net owned funds
    • Ceilings: acceptance of deposits, capital adequacy, credit rating, net-owned funds
    • RBI entrusted with comprehensive supervisory system
    • Statutory auditors must report all non-compliance

    Unit II

    Principles for Effective Regulation

    7 principles for effective regulation of the financial system

    • 1. Capital & Liquidity: Ensure all FIs have enough capital and liquidity so risk of failure is very low — foster resiliency
    • 2. Senior Management Regulation: Regulatory measures on senior management to prevent detrimental decisions
    • 3. Innovation with Restriction: Facilitate innovation — financial products must comply with rules and regulations
    • 4. International Standards: Ensure international standards are followed
    • 5. Effective Resolution Regime: Allow insolvent firms to fail without threatening the national financial system and without taxpayer support
    • 6. Prudent Use of Resources: Financial resources used in the most prudent and effective way — efficiency and economy
    • 7. Strict Supervision: Strict supervision on the financial market to minimise harmful effects of competition
    Unit III

    Mutual Funds

    Meaning, structure, classification, investment modes

    Definition

    • Professionally managed collective investment scheme that pools money from many investors
    • Invests in stocks, bonds, short-term money market instruments and other securities
    • Fund manager invests on behalf of investors by buying/selling stocks, bonds etc.
    • Investors are joint beneficial owners in the trust

    Why MFs? (Problems with Direct Investment)

    • Spending time to find company performance
    • Understanding future business prospects
    • Finding track record of promoters
    • Analysing dividend and bonus issue history
    • MFs solve all these — professional management
    Classification of Mutual Funds

    Open Ended Funds

    • No fixed maturity date
    • Accept continuous sale and re-purchase requests
    • Transactions are NAV-based
    • Unit capital is not fixed

    Closed Ended Funds

    • Run for a specific period
    • Offered in an NFO (New Fund Offer) but closed for further purchases after NFO
    • Unit capital is kept constant

    Interval Funds

    • Variant of closed-ended funds
    • Becomes open-ended at specific intervals
    • Have to be mandatorily listed

    Debt Funds

    • Invest in short and long-term debt instruments
    • Aim to provide regular income

    Equity Funds

    • Invest in equity securities
    • Aim to provide growth and capital appreciation over long term

    Hybrid Funds

    • Invest in combination of equity and debt securities
    • Proportion of equity and debt may vary
    • Aim to provide for both income and capital appreciation

    Passive Funds

    • Replicate a market index
    • Invest in same securities and same proportion as index
    • No active selection of any stock/sector
    • Lower expenses; portfolio modified when index composition changes

    Active Funds

    • Invest in securities and sectors that may offer better return than index
    • Actively manage allocation to market securities and cash
    • May perform better or worse than the market index
    • Incur higher cost than passive funds

    Lump-sum Investment

    • One time investment
    • Usually large sum invested in one go
    • Investor faces risk of volatility in markets

    Systematic Investment Plan (SIP)

    • Staggered investment
    • Period: 6 months, 1/3/5 years
    • Specific intervals: monthly, quarterly, half-yearly
    • Made on specific dates: e.g. 1st, 5th, 10th, 15th of every month

    Growth Option

    • Gains made in portfolio are retained and reflected in NAV
    • Realized profit/loss treated as capital gains or loss
    • No increase in units except if investor purchases/sells

    Dividend Payout Option

    • Fund declares dividend from realized profits
    • Amount and frequency varies; depends on distributable surplus
    • NAV falls after dividend payout to the extent of dividend paid

    Dividend Reinvestment Option

    • Dividend is re-invested in same scheme by buying additional units at ex-dividend NAV
    • Number of units increases each time a dividend is declared and reinvested

    Definition

    • Deals mainly with trading of corporate bond issues

    • Most dominant category in the Indian debt market

    Bond Market

    • Financial Institutions bonds
    • Corporate bonds and debentures
    • Public Sector Units (PSU) bonds
    • Bonds issued to meet financial requirements at a fixed cost
    Debt Instruments

    Government Securities

    • Issued by RBI on behalf of Government of India
    • Maturity period: 1 to 30 years
    • Fixed interest rate; interests payable semi-annually
    • T-Bills: issued by RBI for 91, 182, and 364 days
    • Dated G-Secs: fixed or floating coupon; tenor 5 to 40 years

    Corporate Bonds

    • From PSUs and private corporations
    • Extensive range of tenures up to 15 years; also some perpetual bonds
    • Higher risk than G-Secs; depend on corporation, industry, market conditions, rating
    • Higher returns than G-Secs

    Other Instruments

    • Certificate of Deposit (CD)
    • Commercial Papers (CP)
    • (Details under Unit I Money Market)

    Price-Yield Relationship of Bond

    • Price and yield relationship between bonds is not straight line but convex
    • When yield increases, price falls (inverse relationship)
    • The convexity means the price drop from yield increase is less than the price rise from equal yield decrease
    Unit III

    Derivatives Market

    Types, features, participants, functions, uses

    Definition

    • Financial instrument whose value is derived from value of an underlying asset
    • Underlying asset: securities, commodities, bullion, currency, livestock, interest rates, stock index, forex rate
    • No independent value — entirely derived from underlying asset
    • Two types: Commodity derivatives and Financial derivatives

    Features of Financial Derivatives

    • It is a contract
    • Specified obligation
    • Direct or exchange traded
    • Related to notional amount
    • Delivery of underlying asset not involved
    • May be used as deferred delivery
    • Secondary market instruments
    • Exposure to risk

    Forward Contracts

    • Customised contract between two entities
    • Settlement takes place on a specific date in the future at today's pre-agreed price
    • Negotiated between individual parties (not standardised)

    Futures Contracts

    • Agreement to buy or sell an asset at a certain time in the future at a certain price
    • Special types of forward contracts that are standardised and exchange-traded
    • NSE commenced trading in derivatives with launch of index futures on June 12, 2000
    • Index Options launched June 4, 2001; Options on individual securities July 2, 2001
    • Futures on individual securities introduced November 9, 2001

    Commodity Exchanges in India

    MCX — Multi Commodity ExchangeNCDEX — National Commodity and Derivatives ExchangeICEX — Indian Commodity ExchangeNMCE — National Multi Commodity ExchangeNSE and BSE (since 2018)

    Options

    • Give the right but not the obligation to buy or sell
    • Call Option: Right but not obligation to buy a given quantity at a given price on or before a given future date
    • Put Option: Right but not obligation to sell a given quantity at a given price on or before a given date
    • Longer-dated options are called warrants and are generally traded over-the-counter

    Swaps

    • Private agreements between two parties to exchange cash flows in the future according to a prearranged formula
    • Can be regarded as portfolios of forward contracts
    • Interest Rate Swaps: Swapping only the interest related cash flows in the same currency
    • Currency Swaps: Swapping both principal and interest in different currencies

    Hedgers

    • Bears an economic risk and uses futures market to reduce that risk
    • Hedging is a prudent business practice
    • Prudent manager has a legal duty to understand and use futures market hedging mechanism

    Speculators

    • Accepts the risk the hedger does not want to take
    • Believes potential return outweighs the risk
    • Primary purpose of derivatives markets is not speculation — they permit transfer of risk

    Arbitrageurs

    • Actively seek out minor pricing discrepancies
    • Keep prices in the marketplace efficient
    • Efficient market: securities priced in accordance with perceived risk and potential return

    Functions of Derivatives Markets

    • Discovery of price: Prices reflect future perceptions of market participants; lead prices of underlying assets to perceived future level
    • Risk transfer: Derivatives market helps to transfer risks
    • Linked to cash markets: Derivatives are linked to underlying cash markets; with introduction of derivatives, underlying market witnesses higher trading volumes
    • Check on speculation: Speculation traders shift to a more controlled environment of derivatives market

    Uses of Derivatives

    • Risk aversion tools: Control, avoid, shift and manage different types of risks through hedging, arbitraging, spreading
    • Prediction of future prices: Serve as barometers of future price trends; discovery of new prices on spot and futures markets
    • Enhance liquidity: Based on margin trading — no immediate full amount required; large number of traders operate, enhancing liquidity and reducing transaction costs
    • Assist investors: Help investors, traders and managers of large funds to devise strategies for proper asset allocation and investment goals
    • Integration of price structure: Smoothen price fluctuations, squeeze price spread, remove gluts and shortages
    • Catalyse growth of financial markets: Encourage competitive trading; increase trading volume in the country

    Unit V

    Credit Cards

    Definition, history, transaction process, advantages and disadvantages

    Definition

    • Payment card issued by a bank/financial services company allowing cardholders to borrow funds to pay for goods and services
    • Cardholders must pay back borrowed money plus applicable interest
    • Credit card issuer may also grant a separate cash line of credit (LOC)

    History

    • Use of credit cards originated in USA during the 1920s — oil companies and hotel chains began issuing to customers
    • 1949: Frank McNamara invented the concept — led to Diners Club Card
    • Started with 27 restaurants, 200 members; by 1953 became first internationally accepted charge card
    • 1958: American Express introduced travel and entertainment card
    • 1959: American Express introduced embossed plastic cards
    • 1981: Credit card market in India started when Visa issued the card
    • Andhra Bank was pioneer of credit cards in India
    Key Players in Credit Card Transaction
    • Cardholder: User who obtains credit card from issuing bank; presents card to pay for goods/services
    • Merchant: Business that maintains merchant account to accept credit cards
    • Acquiring Bank (Merchant's Bank): Provides merchants with equipment and software; deposits funds from credit card sales into merchant's account
    • Issuing Bank (Cardholder Bank): Pays acquiring banks for purchases cardholders make; cardholder repays issuing bank
    • Card Associations (Visa and MasterCard): Govern members; set interchange fees and qualification guidelines; act as arbiter between banks; maintain and improve card network

    Authorization Process

    • 1. Cardholder begins transaction
    • 2. Merchant transmits the transaction info
    • 3. Acquirer/Processor captures and routes info to the Card Network
    • 4. Visa/MasterCard Network (BankNet/VisaNet) routes between banks
    • 5. Issuer approval
    • 6. Visa/MasterCard Network sends response to Acquirer
    • 7. Merchant receives approval info

    Clearing & Settlement

    • 1. Merchant sends batch of approved authorizations to Acquirer
    • 2. Acquiring bank reconciles and transmits info via the Card Network
    • 3. Card Network debits the issuing bank's account and credits the acquiring bank's account for net amount; deducts interchange and network fees
    • 4. The card issuing bank pays the acquiring bank
    • 5. Cardholder is responsible for repaying purchase amount and interests to Issuing Bank

    Repayment

    • At end of billing cycle, card issuer sends statement showing all transactions, previous balance, new balance, minimum payment due and due date
    • Grace period: Period between date of purchase and due date listed on statement
    • If bill paid in full by due date during grace period — no interest charges accrue
    • If balance carried forward — interest is charged
    • APR (Annual Percentage Rate) reflects cost of carrying a balance on annualised basis; includes interest rate and other costs (annual fee etc.)

    Advantages

    • Easy access to credit: Deferred payment basis — use now, pay later
    • Building a line of credit: Banks use credit card usage to gauge loan applicant's creditworthiness
    • EMI facility: Pay off purchases in equated monthly installments
    • Incentives and offers: Discounts on flight tickets, holidays, large purchases
    • Flexible credit: 45–60 days free short-term credit if full balance paid by due date
    • Record of expenses: Detailed list sent with monthly statement
    • Purchase protection: Insurance for card purchases that might be lost, damaged or stolen

    Disadvantages

    • Minimum due trap: Card holders deceived into thinking minimum amount is total due — in fact it is the least amount expected
    • Hidden costs: Late payment fees, joining fees, renewal fees, processing fees
    • Ease of overuse: Revolving credit can lead to overspending and cycle of debt
    • High interest rate: Average rate of 3% per month = 36% per annum
    • Credit card fraud: Possible to clone a card and gain access to confidential information

    Credit Card Fraud & Cyber Crime

    • Credit card fraud = using a credit card to obtain money or goods fraudulently
    • Nilson Report 2020: Card Fraud Losses reached $28.65 Billion
    • By 2025: total payment card volume worldwide projected to be $56.182 trillion
    • Gross card fraud globally expected to be $35.31 billion
    Unit V

    Asset Securitisation

    Definition, genesis, elements, players, benefits

    Definition

    • Process of pooling and repackaging of homogeneous illiquid financial assets into marketable securities that can be sold to investors

    Genesis

    • Severe financial crisis faced by certain states in US during 1969–70
    • Federal government restriction on inter-state lending and borrowing
    • Raised funds from surplus states by issuing instruments backed by mortgaged properties
    • Helped bankers to survive in the market and make additional earnings

    Why Securitisation?

    • Provide efficient access to capital markets
    • Minimise issuer-specific limitations on ability to raise capital
    • Convert illiquid assets to cash
    • Diversify and target funding sources, investor base and transaction structures
    • Raise capital to generate additional assets
    • Raise capital without prospectus-type disclosure
    • Transfer risk to third parties
    3 Elements of Securitisation
    • 1. Conversion of existing illiquid assets (loans, advances, receivables) into tradable security
    • 2. Making portfolio of assets to issue securities — called reference portfolio
    • 3. Reconverting them into fresh assets through capital market operations
    Players and their Roles

    Originator

    • Entity making loans to borrowers or having receivables from customers

    SPV (Special Purpose Vehicle)

    • Buys assets from Originator and packages them into security for further sale
    • Separates risk of assets from credit risk of the seller
    • Bankruptcy remote entity

    Other Players

    • Credit Rating Agency: Provides value addition to security
    • Insurance Company/Underwriters: Cover against redemption risk to investor and/or under-subscription
    • Obligors: Whose debts and collateral constitute underlying assets
    • Investor: Party to whom securities are sold

    Eligible Collaterals — Must be Homogeneous in:

    Underlying assetsMaturity periodCash flow profile
    Eligible Loans
    Housing financeTerm loan financeCar loanCredit card receivablesExport credit

    7 Benefits of Securitisation

    • Separates credit risk of assets from credit risk of Originator
    • Lower cost of borrowing for Originator
    • Illiquid assets converted into marketable securities — alternate funding source
    • Remove assets from balance sheet → improve capital adequacy
    • Increase operations in particular portfolio without increasing total exposure
    • Creates highly diversified portfolio in terms of assets and geography
    • Dependability of cash flows from assets as signified by ageing of portfolio
    Unit V

    Credit Rating

    Meaning, nature, types, factors analyzed, rating systems

    Meaning

    • Opinion on the relative degree of risk associated with timely payment of interest and principal on a debt instrument
    • Assessment of an entity's ability to pay its financial obligations
    • Based on financial history and current assets and liabilities
    • Determined by Credit Rating Agencies
    • Tells lender/investor the probability of the subject being able to pay back a loan

    Nature of Credit Rating

    • Rating is based on information
    • Many factors affect rating: Quality of Management, Corporate Strategy, International Environment
    • Rating by more than one agency
    • Publication of ratings

    Types

    • Sovereign Credit Rating: Sovereign entity; risk level of investing environment; used by investors looking to invest abroad; takes political risk into account
    • Corporate Credit Rating: Financial indicator to potential investors of debt securities such as bonds
    6 Factors Analyzed for Rating

    1. Business Risk Analysis

    • Country and macroeconomic risk
    • Industry risk
    • Competitive position (Market position, Diversification, Operating efficiency, Ownership/governance, Profitability)

    2. Financial Analysis

    • Accounting
    • Cash flow adequacy
    • Financial governance and policies/risk tolerance
    • Liquidity/Short-term factors

    3. Management Evaluation

    • Management goals, plans and strategies
    • Capacity to overcome unfavorable conditions
    • Staff experience and skills, planning and control system

    4. Geographical Analysis

    • Diversification
    • Subsidies from the government
    • Undertaken to determine locational advantages

    5. Regulatory & Competitive Environment

    • Evaluate impact of regulation/deregulation on the issuer company

    6. Fundamental Analysis

    • Liquidity management
    • Profitability and financial position
    • Interest and tax rates sensitivity of the company

    Rating System

    • Usually expressed in alphabetical or alphanumeric symbols
    • Symbols help differentiate between debt instruments on basis of underlying credit quality
    • International agencies: Fitch IBCA, Moody's, Standard & Poor's — sell credit rating analysis
    • Focus on default risk, not exchange rate risk
    • Assessing sovereign debt focuses on political risk and economic risk
